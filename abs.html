Hazard & Handling

Combustible polymer; dust control and thermal fume management

ABS is typically supplied as pellets/granules. It can burn and produce irritating smoke; dust accumulation should be minimized. During processing (high temperature), avoid fume inhalation; use local exhaust ventilation and keep ignition sources away from dust/volatiles.

Chronic Health Notes

Repeated dust/fume exposure may irritate airways; molten polymer burns

Long-term workplace hygiene focuses on limiting dust and thermal decomposition fumes from processing. Use gloves/eye protection during handling, and avoid skin contact with molten polymer. Maintain housekeeping—especially important in high-throughput compounding and in large Asia/China conversion hubs where scale amplifies dust and fume management needs.

Production Process & Capacity

ABS is produced by graft polymerization of styrene and acrylonitrile onto polybutadiene rubber , followed by recovery, compounding and pelletizing. China is a major demand-and-conversion hub (appliances/electronics), shaping global capacity additions and grade development.
1Feedstock & Rubber Latex
Styrene and acrylonitrile monomers are prepared; polybutadiene rubber (often latex) provides the impact-toughening phase.
2Graft Polymerization
SAN chains are formed and grafted onto rubber particles, producing a toughened morphology. Process route (emulsion/mass/suspension) shapes particle size and performance.
3Recovery & Devolatilization
Polymer is recovered, washed/dried, and residual monomers/volatiles are reduced. This step is critical for odor and low-residual specs.
4Compounding & Pelletizing
Stabilizers, pigments, flame-retardants (by grade), and modifiers are compounded; then extruded and pelletized for converters—highly scaled in Asia/China appliance & electronics clusters.
