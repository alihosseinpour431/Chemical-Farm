Hazard Classification

Flammable liquid/vapor; explosive mixtures; polymerization hazard

Styrene is a volatile aromatic monomer. Keep away from heat/sparks/flames and provide good ventilation. Because styrene can self-polymerize (exothermic), it is typically stored with inhibitor (e.g., TBC) and temperature control; avoid contamination and do not distill to dryness.

Chronic Health Effects

Repeated exposure may affect CNS/hearing; suspected carcinogenicity (regulatory)

Repeated inhalation exposure to styrene vapors may cause neurological effects (e.g., fatigue, concentration issues) and is associated with hearing impacts in occupational settings. Follow SDS limits, minimize inhalation, and use goggles/gloves; ensure closed handling and vapor recovery where feasible.

Production Process & Capacity

Styrene is typically produced via ethylbenzene (EB) production + EB dehydrogenation , followed by purification with inhibitor-managed storage. China is a key growth center, with many new units integrated downstream into PS/ABS and coastal petrochemical hubs.
1Ethylbenzene (EB) Synthesis
Benzene reacts with ethylene (alkylation) to form ethylbenzene. Modern units emphasize high selectivity, low by-products, and integration with refinery/petrochemical benzene/ethylene sources.
2Dehydrogenation (EB → Styrene)
Ethylbenzene is catalytically dehydrogenated to styrene at elevated temperature (often with steam dilution). Reactor/catalyst performance drives conversion, selectivity, and energy intensity.
3Purification & Recycle
Distillation separates styrene from unreacted EB (recycle), light ends, and heavies. Inhibitor management is critical to prevent runaway polymerization in columns and storage.
4Safe Storage & Logistics
Because SM is flammable and polymerizable, storage typically uses nitrogen blanketing, temperature control, inhibitor dosing, vapor control, and strict grounding/bonding during transfer.
