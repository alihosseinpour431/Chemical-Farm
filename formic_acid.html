Hazard Classification

Flammable; corrosive to skin/eyes; toxic if inhaled

Formic acid is used widely in leather/textile processing, rubber coagulation, and as a preservative (e.g., silage/animal feed). Concentrated solutions are strongly corrosive and can cause severe skin burns and eye damage; inhalation of vapors/mists can be hazardous and requires effective ventilation. Keep away from ignition sources, avoid aerosol formation, and use chemical-resistant gloves, goggles/face shield, and compatible acid-rated materials.

Chronic Health Effects

Repeated exposure risk (respiratory irritation; dermatitis)

Repeated exposure to vapors/mists may contribute to persistent respiratory irritation; prolonged skin contact can lead to dermatitis and chemical burns depending on concentration. Use closed transfer where possible, control mists during spraying/cleaning, and apply occupational exposure controls (local exhaust ventilation, eyewash/shower readiness, and acid-compatible PPE).

Production Process & Capacity

Formic acid is typically produced via methyl formate synthesis + hydrolysis followed by dehydration/purification to commercial grades.
1 Feedstock Preparation
Methanol, carbon monoxide (CO), and water are prepared; corrosion control and materials selection are critical for acid service.
2 Methyl Formate Formation
Methanol is carbonylated with CO to produce methyl formate; selectivity and recycle loops determine overall efficiency.
3 Hydrolysis & Separation
Methyl formate is hydrolyzed to formic acid and methanol; distillation/extraction de-bottlenecks water–acid separation and recycles methanol.
4 Grade Finishing & Logistics
Dehydration sets commercial grades (e.g., 85–99%); storage/shipping require acid-compatible tanks, ventilation, and flammability controls.

Global Demand Analysis

Demand is driven primarily by animal feed preservation (silage), leather tanning, textile dyeing/finishing, and rubber processing. Consumption is concentrated in Asia due to large leather/textile and industrial processing bases, while Europe remains important in feed and specialty applications.
