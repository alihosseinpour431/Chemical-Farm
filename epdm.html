Hazard Classification

Solid EPDM is commonly supplied as non-hazardous; manage dust/thermal fumes in processing

Many EPDM grades are not classified as hazardous under GHS in supplier SDS. In compounding and high-temperature processing (mixing/extrusion/vulcanization), ensure local exhaust ventilation; avoid inhalation of fumes. If EPDM is ground to fine powder, treat as combustible dust risk and control ignition sources.

Chronic Health Effects

Dust irritation (if present); thermal processing fumes may irritate

Routine handling of solid EPDM has low exposure risk. In manufacturing settings, apply industrial hygiene: ventilation, housekeeping for dust, and PPE as needed (gloves, safety glasses).

Production Process & Capacity

EPDM is produced by copolymerizing ethylene + propylene with a small diene fraction to introduce controlled unsaturation for vulcanization. Production commonly uses Ziegler–Natta or metallocene catalysts in solution/slurry polymerization, followed by finishing (bale/pellet) and optional compounding.
1 Monomer Purification
Ethylene/propylene are dried and purified; diene (e.g., ENB/DCPD/VNB) is conditioned to control catalyst poisons and moisture.
2 Catalytic Polymerization
Copolymerization in solution/slurry reactors produces EPDM with target ethylene content, molecular weight, and diene level (unsaturation).
3 Deactivation & Solvent/Monomer Recovery
Catalyst is quenched; unreacted monomers/solvent are separated and recycled to improve yield and economics.
4 Finishing (Bale/Pellet) & Compounding
Polymer is dried and formed into bales/pellets. For end-use, EPDM is often compounded with fillers, oils, curatives, and additives.
