Hazard Classification

Oxidizing gas; may intensify fire (H270) + gas under pressure (H280)

Oxygen is not flammable, but it strongly supports combustion. Keep away from oils/grease and combustible materials. Use oxygen-clean equipment, open valves slowly (adiabatic compression risk), and ensure good ventilation—especially to avoid oxygen-enriched atmospheres.

Chronic Health Effects

High oxygen exposure (especially elevated partial pressure) can cause oxygen toxicity

Long or high-dose exposure (not typical at normal workplace concentrations) may irritate lungs and, at elevated pressures, affect the CNS. For liquid oxygen (LOX), repeated contact risk is cryogenic burns/frostbite. Controls focus on preventing oxygen enrichment and maintaining clean, compatible materials.

Production Process & Capacity

Industrial oxygen is produced mainly by cryogenic air separation units (ASU) and, for smaller/onsite needs, by VPSA/PSA adsorption systems .
1Air Compression & Purification
Ambient air is compressed and purified (removal of moisture/CO2/hydrocarbons) to protect cold-box equipment and ensure stable operation.
2Cryogenic Distillation / Separation
The purified air is cooled and separated in distillation columns to produce oxygen (and co-products like nitrogen/argon).
3Product Compression / Storage
Oxygen is delivered as compressed gas, pipeline supply, or liquid oxygen (LOX) in insulated tanks depending on logistics and user demand.
4Oxygen-Safe Handling & QA
Because oxygen intensifies fire, oxygen-clean materials, strict incompatibility controls (oil/grease), and QA on purity/moisture are essential.

Global Demand Analysis

Industrial oxygen demand is anchored by steel, chemicals, and refining utilities. Asia-Pacific leads volume, with China as a major driver due to large steel capacity, coal-to-chemicals projects, and expanding on-site ASU/pipeline systems.
